# Glocal — Cultural Snippet Content Guidelines

## Purpose

Cultural snippets in Glocal are designed to gently enrich time-based announcements with:

- cultural context
- language exposure
- reflective awareness of daily life across regions

They are not meant to educate in depth or overwhelm the user.

---

## 1. Length Standard

- Ideal: **8–18 words**
- Maximum: **24 words** (except rare observance lines)

### Good
In many Nigerian homes, evening is a time for dinner and conversation.

### Too long
In many Nigerian households, the evening period is often associated with meal preparation, social interaction, and relaxation after a long day.

---

## 2. Tone and Style

Use a tone that is:

- observational
- calm
- respectful
- lightly expressive

### Preferred wording
- "often"
- "can be"
- "in many places"
- "in many homes"

### Avoid
- "always"
- "everyone"
- sweeping generalizations
- authoritative or absolute claims

---

## 3. Cultural Accuracy

Avoid reducing regions or cultures to a single trait.

### Good
In many Spanish cities, evening meals often begin later.

### Risky
Spaniards eat very late every night.

---

## 4. Speech Suitability (TTS)

Snippets must sound natural when spoken:

- use simple sentence structure
- avoid complex punctuation
- avoid brackets or slashes
- avoid long clauses

### Good
In Japanese, Ohayo gozaimasu is a common morning greeting.

### Poor for speech
Ohayo-gozaimasu, i.e., a culturally appropriate salutation, is contextually used in Japanese-speaking environments.

---

## 5. Merge Compatibility

Every snippet must work after a time statement.

### Example
It is 7 PM. In many Nigerian homes, evening is a time for dinner and conversation.

If it does not flow naturally, rewrite it.

---

## 6. Independence Rule

Each snippet must stand alone.

### Avoid
As mentioned earlier, evenings are social here.

### Prefer
In many Senegalese settings, evening can be a social hour.

---

## 7. Region Sensitivity

Avoid:

- stereotypes
- exoticizing language
- overgeneralization
- reductionist descriptions

Prefer:

- daily rhythm
- greetings
- city pace
- household patterns
- time-of-day behavior

---

## 8. Language Snippet Guidelines

Language snippets should be:

- short
- common
- easy to pronounce
- optionally translated

### Good
In Yoruba, E kaaro is a common morning greeting.

### Avoid
- long grammar explanations
- multiple unfamiliar phrases in one line

---

## 9. Observance Guidelines

Observances should be:

- rare
- date-driven
- globally or regionally meaningful
- calm in tone

### Good
Today is World Mental Health Day, a reminder of emotional well-being.

### Avoid
Today is a very important day that everyone should celebrate enthusiastically.

---

## 10. Talkativeness Mode Compatibility

### Minimal
- no cultural snippets

### Balanced
- 1–2 snippets per day
- attach to existing announcements only

### Expressive
- up to 3 snippets per day
- includes language and reflective snippets

---

## 11. Repetition Avoidance

Avoid near-duplicate meanings:

### Bad pattern
- Evening is a time to relax
- Evening is a time to rest
- Evening is a time to unwind

### Good variation
- family rhythm
- city activity
- greetings
- reflection

---

## 12. Pronunciation Safety

When using non-English words:

- keep them short
- use clear romanization
- avoid multiple foreign phrases in one line

---

## 13. Content Categories

Maintain a balance across:

- `daily_life`
- `language`
- `city_life`
- `reflection`
- `observance`

---

## 14. Quality Checklist

Before adding a snippet, confirm:

- [ ] Is it under 24 words?
- [ ] Is it respectful and non-absolute?
- [ ] Does it sound natural when spoken?
- [ ] Can it merge into a time announcement?
- [ ] Does it add unique value?

---

## 15. Engineering Rule (Critical)

Content changes must NOT alter system behavior:

- Do not introduce a new speech pathway
- Do not bypass governance logic
- Do not override quiet hours or suppression rules
- Culture must remain **merge-first**

---

## Final Principle

A Glocal cultural snippet should feel like:

a soft human observation carried by time

Not:

a factual explanation delivered by a machine
